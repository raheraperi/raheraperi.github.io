Cultural Blog - Motivation

4 May 2018

How does life look through the 'pleasure, passion and purpose' lens?

We're conditioned to be pleasure-seeking creatures due to our societal and economic constructs. This has led to a very anxious and depressed society - fueled by the compulsion to seek instant gratification. We may feel happy for a brief moment eating our favourite guilty pleasure, or other bodily pleasures, but this is very fleeting.

Sustainable happiness in life can be achieved by focusing on the stuff that is intrinsically valuable , the deep stuff that drives us to serve a greater cause than ourselves. When we know and understand our higher purpose, we can change the path of our lives/careers to align to this. When we know we are in an environment that enables us to contribute towards our higher purpose the work itself is the reward.

How did you find the resilience ideas and meditation exercise?

In this ever-changing world nothing is certain and without resilience we'd constantly be in a state of anxiety. I like how the book frames resilience as a vehicle to help you overcome your obstacles and to get you where you want to be.

I really liked how the resilience meditation invoked the feelings of failure and success, and enabled the practitioner to assess these thoughts and feelings without judgement. In the past, I have often decided not to take risks because I was so scared of a negative outcome or failing. As the book says, failure should be not only embraced but encouraged. With learning a new skill such as coding, no-one is going to be an overnight master. We should reflect on our failures and successes and use them as invaluable learnings.
